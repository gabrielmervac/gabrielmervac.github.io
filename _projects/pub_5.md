**Preprint, 2023**

In many random search scenarios — animals foraging for ephemeral food patches, rescue operations locating survivors, or diffusing molecules seeking transient binding sites — the target is not permanent but has a finite lifetime. This paper presents exact results for a minimal model in which a one-dimensional Lévy flight searcher must find a target of random lifetime. The target, fixed at the origin, dies at each discrete time step with probability 1 − a (geometric lifetime distribution). Two key observables are studied: the capture probability and the conditional mean first passage time (CMFT) to find the target while it is still alive.

In stark contrast to the case of an immortal target — where capture probability is always unity and the mean first passage time is always infinite in one dimension, regardless of the Lévy index — both quantities become non-trivial functions of the Lévy index μ when the target has a finite lifetime. Crucially, both can be simultaneously optimized by choosing an appropriate μ. The optimal Lévy index μ* exhibits an abrupt transition as the initial distance x₀ to the target is varied. This transition is discontinuous (first-order) for short target lifetimes and continuous (second-order) for long lifetimes, with the two regimes separated by a tricritical point in the (x₀, a) phase diagram at a₁ = 2e^((√15−2)/11) ≈ 0.9257.

These results reveal new mechanisms by which Lévy search strategies gain advantages in uncertain environments. The analysis relies on the Pollaczek–Spitzer formula for the generating function of the survival probability of Lévy flights, and the phase transitions are determined by the coexistence and merging of local minima in the capture probability as a function of μ. The findings suggest that the heavy-tailed nature of Lévy flights provides a qualitative advantage specifically in the search for non-permanent targets, with optimal strategies that differ fundamentally from those applicable to immortal targets.
