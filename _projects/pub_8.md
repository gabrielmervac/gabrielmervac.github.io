**Physical Review E, 2021**

Run-and-tumble (RT) motion — consisting of straight-line "runs" at constant speed interrupted by random direction-reversing "tumbles" — is the canonical mode of locomotion for many motile bacteria and active particles. This paper studies the first hitting time statistics when a one-dimensional run-and-tumble particle searches for a target site that itself switches stochastically between visible (absorbing) and invisible (non-absorbing) states, independently of the particle's motion.

Exact expressions for the mean first hitting time (MFHT) are derived for motion in a finite domain with reflecting boundaries, as a function of the particle's turning rate, the target switching rates, and domain size. A key finding is that fully ballistic motion (zero turning rate) minimizes the mean hitting time — a result that contrasts with the behavior in purely diffusive searches. However, the relative fluctuations (coefficient of variation) of the first hitting time are large and exhibit non-monotonic behavior with respect to both the turning rate and target transition rates. Paradoxically, fluctuations can be largest when the target is visible most of the time, not when it is mostly hidden or rapidly switching.

On the infinite line, the classical t⁻³/² tail of the first hitting time distribution is preceded by an intermediate t⁻¹/² scaling regime due to target intermittency — extending the result previously found for Brownian searchers. This transient regime becomes very long when the target is mostly invisible, especially at low turning rates. In both finite and infinite geometries, the intermittent target problem is connected to partial absorption problems, allowing the effective Robin boundary condition parameter to be expressed in terms of the dynamical rates of the target and the particle's transport properties.
