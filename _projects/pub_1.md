**Preprint, 2026**

Synthesis and degradation of proteins and other molecular structures are ubiquitous in cellular and intercellular processes, from viral replication to quorum sensing in bacteria and pattern formation in multicellular organisms. Despite this prevalence, the reaction kinetics of stochastic synthesis-degradation (SSD) processes — where independent diffusing particles are continuously born and destroyed at constant rates — are not well understood. This work adapts methods from the theory of stochastic resetting to derive the first-passage properties of a collection of independent particles undergoing arbitrary diffusive motion while subject to SSD.

The central result is that, at equal synthesis and degradation rates, the mean reaction time with a target site can be minimized with respect to the common rate, analogously to the optimization seen in stochastic resetting. A coefficient-of-variation (CV) criterion is derived that determines when SSD accelerates the search compared to a single non-degrading particle. When synthesis and degradation rates are treated independently and synthesis costs are incorporated, an optimal synthesis rate is identified. In bounded domains, SSD improves mean search time compared to a single non-degrading particle whenever the synthesis rate exceeds a critical threshold that obeys a universal scaling relation.

These findings are illustrated with Brownian diffusion both on the infinite line and in a bounded interval. A key distinction from stochastic resetting is also established: despite sharing identical steady-state particle densities when synthesis equals degradation, the two processes have fundamentally different first-passage statistics because particle number fluctuates in SSD. This work connects population-level biological dynamics to the statistical physics of first-passage processes, with implications for understanding reaction kinetics in cellular signaling.
