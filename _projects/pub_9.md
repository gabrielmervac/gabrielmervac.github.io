**Journal of Physics A: Mathematical and Theoretical, 2021**

Gated reactions — where a target switches between reactive and non-reactive conformational states — are ubiquitous in biology, appearing in protein binding, gene expression, ion channel transport, and diffusion through synthetic nanopores. This paper combines two features not previously studied together: a stochastically gated target and stochastic resetting of the searcher. A Brownian particle on a semi-infinite line is subject to Poissonian resetting at constant rate r and searches for a target at the origin that switches between reactive (open) and non-reactive (closed) states at constant rates α and β.

An exact expression for the mean first hitting time (MFHT) of the particle to the target in its reactive state is derived via the Laplace transform of the survival probability. The MFHT can be minimized at an optimal resetting rate that depends on the target transition rates in a non-trivial way. When the target relaxation rate is much larger than both the resetting rate and the inverse diffusion time, the system becomes equivalent to a partially absorbing boundary problem. In other regimes, however, the optimal resetting rate is a non-monotonic function of the target transition rates — a feature absent in the partial absorption limit and not observed in simpler resetting problems.

The relative fluctuations of the first hitting time are also computed. The universal behavior at optimal resetting (where the coefficient of variation equals unity for perfect targets) breaks down in the presence of target gating, and the fluctuations can take much larger values. This breakdown occurs because the target state evolves independently of the resetting process, unlike models where resetting also reinitializes the target state. The work clarifies the distinct roles of target dynamics and particle dynamics in gated reaction kinetics and provides design principles for search optimization in fluctuating environments.
