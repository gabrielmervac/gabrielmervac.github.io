**Physical Review Letters, 2019**

Many biological processes are governed by the time required for a diffusing molecule to find a target site that is not always accessible — gene regulatory sites that are hidden by chromatin folding, ion channels that stochastically open and close, or DNA-binding proteins occluded by other molecules. This paper studies the first hitting time statistics of a one-dimensional Brownian particle searching for a target that switches stochastically between visible (absorbing) and hidden phases. The mean duration of each phase is set by two independent rates, and the particle is absorbed only when it reaches the target in the visible state.

The survival probability and first hitting time density (FHTD) are derived exactly in Laplace space. Two distinct scaling regimes emerge at large times. At high crypticity — when the target is mostly invisible (β/α ≫ 1) — an unexpected intermediate power-law regime appears in the FHTD, scaling as t⁻¹/² rather than the classical t⁻³/² of a steady absorbing target. This rate-limited regime can span many decades of time and severely broadens the distribution of hitting times. Only at much longer times does the standard t⁻³/² scaling eventually emerge.

A key analytical result is that the intermittent target problem admits an asymptotic mapping onto diffusion with a mixed (Robin) boundary condition, where the effective absorption coefficient κ is determined by the target switching rates as κ = (α/β)√(α+β)D. This mapping rigorously connects a dynamical, two-state target problem to the well-studied theory of partially absorbing boundaries, extending the relevance of that theory to fluctuating biophysical systems. The results are robust to non-Markovian target dynamics and to anomalous (Lévy) diffusion of the searcher.
