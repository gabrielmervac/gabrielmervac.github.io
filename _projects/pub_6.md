**Journal of Physics A: Mathematical and Theoretical, 2018**

Animal foragers in patchy environments face two competing demands: the need to explore large areas when prey are scarce, and the tendency to return to known profitable sites (site fidelity). This paper investigates how these behaviors jointly influence predator–prey population dynamics by incorporating Lévy flight dispersal and stochastic resetting into a Lotka–Volterra model. Predators move randomly according to a power-law (Lévy) dispersal kernel and are subject to stochastic resetting to the prey patch, which models home-range and memory effects. Prey are confined to a single spatial patch.

The Lotka–Volterra rate equations governing the evolution of the two species' densities are solved analytically in the long-time limit. Fixing demographic parameters and the Lévy exponent, there exists an optimal resetting rate that maximizes total predator abundance — achieving a compromise between overexploitation of the habitat (high resetting rate) and underutilization through excessive dispersal away from the patch (low resetting rate). Similarly, at a fixed resetting rate, an optimal Lévy exponent exists that maximizes predator abundance.

These analytical findings are supported by two-dimensional stochastic simulations. A key ecological consequence is that the combined effects of anomalous diffusion and resetting can substantially extend the region of species coexistence in ecosystems facing resource scarcity, compared to models with purely Brownian dispersal. The work thus links the statistical physics of stochastic processes with resetting to concrete predictions about population persistence and coexistence in spatially structured environments.
