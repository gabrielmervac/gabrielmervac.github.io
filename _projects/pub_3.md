**Journal of Statistical Mechanics: Theory and Experiment, 2022**

Stochastic resetting is known to reduce mean first passage times for diffusing particles, but its experimental realization remains challenging because particles cannot be instantaneously teleported back to a fixed position. This paper proposes and analyzes a physically realizable alternative: a Brownian particle subject to an arbitrary confining potential v(x) that is switched on and off intermittently at fixed rates. During "on" phases the particle is attracted toward the potential minimum; during "off" phases it diffuses freely. The particle is constrained between an absorbing wall at the origin and a reflective wall.

For potentials of the form v(x) = k|x − x₀|ⁿ/n with n > 0, the switch-on and switch-off rates that minimize the mean first passage time (MFPT) undergo a continuous phase transition as the potential stiffness k is varied. Above a critical stiffness k_c, potential intermittency enhances target encounter: the minimal MFPT falls below the Kramers time and is achieved at non-vanishing switching rates. The paper focuses on the harmonic case n = 2, extending previous results for the piecewise linear (V-shaped, n = 1) potential in unbounded domains to the practically relevant bounded geometry. A perturbative method valid for general potentials is also developed.

The non-equilibrium stationary states (NESS) emerging in this process are computed exactly for the harmonic potential and analyzed across various limiting regimes of the switching rates. The work demonstrates that intermittent trapping provides an experimentally accessible protocol — implementable e.g. with optical tweezers — that captures the essential phenomenology of stochastic resetting while avoiding the requirement of instantaneous relocation, at the cost of introducing new phase transitions not observed in ideal resetting models.
