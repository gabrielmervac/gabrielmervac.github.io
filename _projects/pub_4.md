**Journal of Statistical Mechanics: Theory and Experiment, 2020**

Stochastic resetting — the random interruption of a particle's motion followed by a return to a fixed position — has been shown theoretically to minimize mean first passage times, but is difficult to realize experimentally because it requires instantaneous relocation. This paper introduces and analyzes a physically realistic alternative: a Brownian particle whose dynamics are driven by a piecewise-linear (V-shaped) confining potential V(X) = μ|X| that is switched on and off stochastically. When the potential is on, it attracts the particle toward the origin; when off, the particle diffuses freely. The result is a diffusion process with stochastic resetting toward the origin that is inherently achievable with external traps such as optical tweezers.

The non-equilibrium steady states (NESS) and first-passage properties of this system are derived analytically. The mean time to reach an absorbing target at a fixed distance from the origin can be minimized with respect to both the switch-on and switch-off rates. Crucially, the optimal rates undergo continuous or discontinuous transitions as the potential strength μ is varied across non-trivial threshold values. A discontinuous transition with metastable behavior is also found when optimizing the potential strength at fixed rates — features not present in idealized instantaneous resetting.

Three key differences distinguish this model from prior non-instantaneous resetting studies: the return toward the origin is stochastic (not deterministic), the particle need not be at the potential minimum when the potential is switched off, and the absorbing target is always detectable regardless of the potential state. Together these features generate qualitatively new phenomenology, including metastability and tricritical-like transitions in the optimal protocol, establishing intermittent confining potentials as a rich and experimentally relevant platform for studying resetting physics.
